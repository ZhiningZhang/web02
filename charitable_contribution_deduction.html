The donation deduction (in U.S. tax law, usually called the charitable contribution deduction) is a tax benefit you can claim when you give money, property, or other assets to a qualified charitable organization.

  • Purpose: To encourage charitable giving by lowering your taxable income.
  • Who can claim it: Individuals and businesses that itemize deductions on their tax returns.
  • Effect: The value of your donation is subtracted from your taxable income, which can reduce the tax you owe.

üíµ Key Rules (U.S., 2025)

  1. Qualified Charities Only

    • Must be IRS-recognized nonprofits (often 501(c)(3) organizations).
    • Examples: Red Cross, Salvation Army, universities, religious institutions.
    • Donations to individuals do not qualify.

  2. Deduction Limits

    • Cash donations: generally up to 60% of your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI).
    • Property or appreciated assets: usually limited to 30% of AGI (varies by asset type).

  3. Documentation Required

    • Receipt or bank record for any amount.
    • For gifts of $250 or more, you must have a written acknowledgment from the charity.
    • For non-cash items worth over $5,000, a qualified appraisal is usually required.

  4. Standard Deduction vs.¬†Itemizing

    • You only benefit if your total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction (in 2025: $14,600 single / $29,200 married filing jointly).
