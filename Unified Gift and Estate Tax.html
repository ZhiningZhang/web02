The estate tax and gift tax are really two parts of the same U.S. federal tax system — they work together under what’s called the Unified Gift and Estate Tax. The idea is simple:

The IRS wants to tax large transfers of wealth, whether you give them away while alive (gift tax) or after you die (estate tax).

🧩 How They Connect

  • Shared Lifetime Exemption: There is one combined limit for both taxes. In 2025, it’s $13.61 million per person.

    • Gifts you make during your lifetime above the annual exclusion reduce this lifetime limit.
    • Whatever is left of the limit applies to your estate when you die.

  • Annual Exclusion First: You can give up to $18,000 per person per year (2025) without affecting your lifetime limit.

🔄 Step-by-Step Example

1️⃣ While Alive – Gift Tax

  • You give your daughter $100,000 in 2025.
  • $18,000 is excluded (annual exclusion).
  • The remaining $82,000 counts toward your lifetime exemption.
  • You file a gift tax return (Form 709), but you pay no tax unless your lifetime total exceeds $13.61M.

2️⃣ After Death – Estate Tax

  • When you die, your estate is valued at $12 million.
  • But you already used $2 million of your lifetime exemption on past gifts.
  • That leaves $11.61 million exempt.
  • The excess ($12M – $11.61M = $0.39M) is subject to estate tax (up to 40%).

⚠️ Important Points

  • No “double tax” — the same amount isn’t taxed twice; gifts just reduce the estate exemption.
  • Spousal Transfers — generally unlimited if your spouse is a U.S. citizen.
  • Charitable Transfers — exempt from both taxes.
  • State Taxes — some states have their own separate estate or inheritance taxes with much lower exemptions.
