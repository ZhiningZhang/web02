The intergenerational gift tax you’re referring to is formally called the Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax (GSTT) in U.S. tax law.

It’s essentially an extra layer of tax designed to prevent wealthy individuals from avoiding estate and gift taxes by transferring assets directly to grandchildren (or younger generations) instead of their own children.

📌 Why It Exists

Without this tax, a very wealthy person could:

  1. Leave everything to their children → taxed once by estate tax.
  2. Children then leave it to grandchildren → taxed again.

To avoid two rounds of tax, they could skip the children entirely and give directly to the grandchildren — avoiding one round. The GSTT closes that loophole.

🔍 How It Works

  • Applies to:

    • Gifts or bequests to someone two or more generations younger than you (grandchildren, great-grandchildren, etc.).
    • Also applies if you give to an unrelated person more than 37½ years younger than you.

  • Tax Rate:

    • Flat 40% (same as the top estate/gift tax rate).

  • Exemption:

    • There’s a separate lifetime GSTT exemption — in 2025, it’s $13.61 million, the same amount as the gift/estate exemption.
    • Annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 in 2025) still applies.

📝 Example

  1. You give your granddaughter $1 million in 2025.

  2. $18,000 is excluded (annual gift exclusion).

  3. The remaining $982,000:

    • Counts against your lifetime gift/estate exemption and
    • Counts against your GSTT exemption.

  4. If your GSTT exemption is used up, the remaining amount is taxed at 40% in addition to any gift/estate tax.

🧠 Key Points to Remember

  • It’s not a separate “gift tax” for ordinary gifts — it’s an additional tax that only applies when you skip a generation.
  • GSTT, gift tax, and estate tax can all interact in one transfer.
  • Careful estate planning (like using trusts) is often needed to avoid accidentally triggering GSTT.
